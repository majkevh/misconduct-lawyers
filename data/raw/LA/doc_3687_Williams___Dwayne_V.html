WILLIAMS, DWAYNE
00-DB-128 (06/21/2001) Reprimand Failure to Cooperate with the Disciplinary System Failure to Supervise Office Personnel

LOUISIANA ATTORNEY DISCIPLINARY BOARD

IN RE:  DWAYNE WILLIAMS

NUMBER 00-DB-128

RULING OF THE DISCIPLINARY BOARD

PER CURIAM

This is a disciplinary proceeding based on the filing of a joint petition for consent discipline, after the institution of formal charges, by Dwayne Williams ("Respondent") of Baton Rouge, Louisiana, an attorney licensed to practice law in the State of Louisiana, and the Office of Disciplinary Counsel ("ODC"). For reasons stated below, the Disciplinary Board accepts the petition and orders a public reprimand be issued against Respondent.

PROCEDURAL HISTORY

On October 12, 2000, ODC filed formal charges, consisting of three counts, against Respondent. Despite being personally served with the formal charges on November 21, 2000, Respondent failed to file an answer to the charges. On December 18, 2000, ODC filed a motion to have the factual allegations contained in the formal charges deemed admitted. On January 10, 2001, the chair of Hearing Committee Number 25 granted ODC's motion. By the same order, the parties were given until February 20, 2001 to file written arguments and documentary evidence on the issue of sanctions.

On February 20, 2001, ODC and Respondent filed a joint petition for consent discipline, stipulation of facts, and memorandum in support of the proposed sanction of a public reprimand. On April 26, 2001, a panel of the Disciplinary Board reviewed this matter. Deputy Disciplinary Counsel James T. Daly represented ODC. Respondent did not appear.

STIPULATED FACTS

The stipulation of facts filed in this matter provides as follows:

"During the course of the investigations of investigative matter Nos. 0008606 and 0008695, the Respondent was not cooperative in providing information necessitating the issuance of two subpoenas in order to obtain information from him.

"Also, in investigative matter No. 0010547, the Respondent had employed a suspended attorney in his law office. That suspended attorney was sending correspondence, without supervision by the Respondent, which gave the impression that the suspended attorney was holding himself out as an attorney. No clients were harmed by these actions by Respondent."

PETITION FOR CONSENT DISCIPLINE

In the petition, Respondent admits to violating Rules 8.1(c) and 8.4(g) (failure to cooperate in a disciplinary investigation) as well as Rule 5.3(b) (failure to properly supervise a non-lawyer employee) of the Rules of Professional Conduct. The parties propose that a formal public reprimand be issued to Respondent and that Respondent be required to attend the Ethics School offered by the Practice Assistance Counsel of the Louisiana State Bar Association.

APPLICATION OF FACTORS CONSIDERED IN IMPOSING SANCTIONS

Respondent and ODC have agreed that Respondent has violated Rules 5.3, 8.1(c) and 8.4(g) of the Rules of Professional Conduct.  The Board must accept that Respondent has violated these rules.  In re Lieberman, 95-2628 (La. 6/7/96); 675 So. 2d 272. Given the violations of these rules, the Board is left to determine if the proposed sanction is appropriate.

Louisiana Supreme Court Rule XIX, Section 10(C) states that in imposing a sanction after a finding of lawyer misconduct, the Court or Board shall consider the following factors:

(1) whether the lawyer has violated a duty owed to a client, to the public, to the legal system, or to the profession;

(2) whether the lawyer acted intentionally, knowingly, or negligently;

(3) the amount of actual or potential injury caused by the lawyer's misconduct; and

(4) the existence of any aggravating or mitigating factors.

Based upon a review of the record, the Board finds that by failing to supervise his non-lawyer employee Respondent has negligently violated duties owed to his clients. Additionally, the Board finds that by failing to cooperate in the disciplinary investigation of complaints filed against him Respondent has knowingly and intentionally violated duties owed to the profession.

The parties contend that Respondent's misconduct in this matter caused no harm to Respondent's clients. There is nothing in the record to dispute this contention. However, Respondent's failure to cooperate has resulted in actual injury to the legal profession. An attorney's failure to cooperate unduly delays and burdens the disciplinary process.

Finally, in the petition, the parties assert that the mitigating factor of absence of a prior disciplinary record is present in this matter. The record contains nothing to support a finding that any other aggravating or mitigating factor is present.

ANALYSIS

The Louisiana Supreme Court relies on the ABA's Standards for Imposing Lawyer Sanctions to determine the baseline sanction by determining "the type of duty violated, the lawyer's mental state and the extent of the injury caused; and then adjust[ing] the sanction in accordance with the aggravating and mitigating factors present."  In re Quaid, 646 So. 2d 343, 350 (La. 1994).

Given the facts of this case, the following ABA Standards are applicable. Standard 7.3 provides for reprimand "when a lawyer negligently engages in conduct that is a violation of a duty owed as a professional, and causes injury or potential injury to a client, the public, or the legal system." Standard 7.2 provides for suspension "when a lawyer knowingly engages in conduct that is a violation of a duty owed as a professional, and causes injury or potential injury to a client, the public, or the legal system."

The Board also looks to relevant case law for assistance in determining an appropriate sanction for violation of the Rules of Professional Conduct.  In the matter of In re Cline, 99-2779 (La. 2/29/00), 756 So. 2d 284, the Court imposed a suspension of six months, with three months deferred, followed by two years of supervised probation for an attorney's failure to properly supervise a non-lawyer employee. The Court noted that in matters involving little or no injury the baseline sanction for failure to properly supervise a non-lawyer is a public reprimand, but that Cline's previous disciplinary record justified an upward deviation. In the instant matter, Respondent does not have a prior disciplinary record; therefore, an upward deviation does not appear appropriate.  Moreover, at the oral argument in this matter, Deputy Disciplinary Counsel advised the Board that in only one instance did the suspended attorney who was working for him send the questionable correspondence.

Other relevant cases include: In re Browning, 98-DB-070 (Board ordered public reprimand for attorney's failure to timely pay a third-party medical provider and for attorney's failure to cooperate in the disciplinary investigation); and In re Harrington, 92-2206 (La. 11/30/92), 608 So. 2d 631 (Court ordered public reprimand for attorney's failure to cooperate in disciplinary investigation of complaint filed against him).

The ABA Standards suggest a public reprimand or suspension is appropriate for Respondent's misconduct in this matter. The case law suggests that, after consideration of the facts and circumstances present in this matter, public reprimand is appropriate for Respondent's misconduct. While the Board does not condone Respondent's failure to cooperate in the initial investigation, his agreement to enter into a consent discipline has lessened the delay caused by this initial failure and warrants consideration in the determination of the appropriate sanction in this matter.

RULING

Based on the foregoing, it is ordered that the joint petition for consent discipline be accepted, and that a public reprimand be issued against the Respondent, Dwayne Williams. The Board also orders that Respondent attend the Ethics School offered by the Practice Assistance Counsel of the Louisiana State Bar Association. Finally, the Board orders that all costs and expenses of these proceedings be assessed to Respondent, with legal interest to commence running thirty days from the date of this Ruling until paid.
