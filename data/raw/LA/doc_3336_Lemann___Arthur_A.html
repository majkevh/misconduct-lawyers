LEMANN, ARTHUR A., III
00-DB-084 (01/11/2001)Reprimand  Neglect of Legal Matters

LOUISIANA ATTORNEY DISCIPLINARY BOARD

IN RE:  ARTHUR A. LEMANN III

NUMBER 00-DB-084

RULING OF THE DISCIPLINARY BOARD

This is a disciplinary proceeding based on the filing of a Joint Petition for Consent Discipline (herein "Petition") by the Respondent, Arthur A. Lemann III (herein "Respondent"), of New Orleans, Louisiana, and the Office of Disciplinary Counsel (herein "ODC") prior to the filing of formal charges.  The Board accepts the Joint Petition and orders a public reprimand be issued against Respondent.

PROCEDURAL HISTORY

On July 12, 2000, prior to the filing of Formal Charges, Respondent and the Office of Disciplinary Counsel filed a Joint Petition for Consent Discipline and a Joint Stipulation of Facts. Both parties recommended Respondent receive a public reprimand. On September 1, 2000, Respondent filed a Memorandum in Support of Petition. On September 28, 2000, a panel of the Disciplinary Board reviewed this matter. Deputy Counsel G. Fred Ours represented the ODC. Mr. Robert S. Glass represented Respondent

STIPULATED FACTS

In November of 1996, Respondent undertook the representation of Benjamin Bura Rayburn, Sr. in connection with the criminal charges set forth in a federal grand jury indictment. The fee agreement between the parties consisted of an hourly rate of $275.00, plus expenses, with a cap of $70,000 on the hourly fee. Mr. Rayburn paid Respondent the $70,000 in three installments. However, there was no discussion between the parties regarding the "provisions of the state law to pay defense fees," which had to be authorized by specific act of the Legislature, pursuant to LSA-R.S. 13:5108.3, et seq. In addition, the fee agreement was not in writing.

In February or March of 1997, prior to the criminal trial, Respondent became aware of this statute. At a time near the conclusion of the trial, Respondent may have mentioned this statute to Mr. Rayburn, but this did not result in an agreement or written contract. However, Respondent states he believed in good faith that Mr. Rayburn agreed to change the capped fee to an open-ended hourly fee and that Mr. Rayburn would be indemnified by the State should he be acquitted. Mr. Rayburn denies these allegations. Thereafter, Respondent, consistent with his belief, continued to record and bill his hourly work even though the $70,000.00 cap had been reached.

Following Mr. Rayburn's acquittal in June of 1997, Respondent submitted a final invoice in the amount of $192,359.37. This amount was based upon Respondent's total hours expended in the criminal case, less a credit for the amount paid by Mr. Rayburn.

  In July of 1997, Respondent drafted a proposed lawsuit for declaratory relief against the State of Louisiana, wherein he requested Mr. Rayburn seek payment from the Legislature in the amount of $295,472.39, consisting of $262,359.37 set forth in Respondent's final invoice and an additional $29,053.57 Mr. Rayburn had paid to another attorney who had represented him prior to November of 1996. Although Mr. Rayburn refused to participate in that lawsuit and refused to request that the Legislature pay any amount in excess of the original $70,000 capped fee, he sought and received payment from the State of Louisiana in the amount of $99,053.57, consisting of the amount previously paid to Respondent and the amount previously paid to the other attorney.

In March of 1998, Respondent sued Mr. Rayburn in the 19th Judicial District Court for the $192,359.37 he alleged was due on the renegotiated fee agreement. The parties entered into stipulated facts and the court issued a judgment with written reasons dismissing Respondent's claim.

REQUEST FOR CONSENT DISCIPLINE AND CONCURRENCE BY ODC

 Based upon the Petition and the stipulated facts, ODC asserts that Respondent's conduct violated duties owed to the public, the profession, and his client. Respondent agrees that his conduct violated Rule 1.5(b)[i] (communication of the basis for the fee).  The Petition contains no references to aggravating or mitigating factors.  Respondent tenders a conditional admission to the misconduct in exchange for a public reprimand. ODC concurs with the consent discipline.

 APPLICATION OF FACTORS CONSIDERED IN IMPOSING SANCTIONS

 Louisiana Supreme Court Rule XIX, Section 10(C) states that in imposing a sanction after a finding of lawyer misconduct, the Court or Board shall consider the following factors:

 1. whether the lawyer has violated a duty owed to a client, to the public, to the legal system, or to the profession;

2. whether the lawyer acted intentionally, knowingly, or negligently;

3. the amount of actual or potential injury caused by the lawyer's misconduct; and

4. the existence of any aggravating or mitigating factors.

The Board finds that the Respondent has violated duties owed to the public, the legal system and the profession.  The Respondent's actions in this matter were negligent.  Further, the Board finds that the Respondent's failure to provide Mr. Rayburn with accurate or complete information relating to the basis of the fee charged caused potential injury to his client. The injury caused by the litigation filed by Respondent against Mr. Rayburn included substantial adverse publicity that reflected poorly on the profession.  The parties have also stipulated that any financial injury sustained by Mr. Rayburn as a result of the litigation was resolved and settled to his satisfaction at the time the court's judgment was issued. 

As previously noted, there are no aggravating or mitigating factors specifically outlined in the Petition. However, a review of the record supports a finding that the aggravating factor of 9.22(i) (substantial experience in the practice of law) and mitigating factors of 9.32(a) (absence of a prior disciplinary record), 9.32(e) (cooperative attitude toward proceedings), and 9.32(l) (remorse) are present in this matter.

ANALYSIS

The Respondent and ODC have agreed that Respondent has violated Rule 1.5(b) of the Rules of Professional Conduct.  The Board must accept that Respondent has violated this rule.  In re Lieberman, 95-2628 (La. 6/7/96); 675 So. 2d 272. Given the violation of Rule 1.5(b), the Board is left to determine if the proposed sanction of a public reprimand is appropriate.  The Louisiana Supreme Court relies on the ABA's Standards for Imposing Lawyer Sanctions to determine the baseline sanction by "the type of duty violated, the lawyer's mental state and the extent of the injury caused; and then adjust the sanction in accordance with the aggravating and mitigating factors present."  In re Quaid, 94-1316 (La. 11/30/94); 646 So.2d 343, 350.

The proposed sanction is in line with the ABA guidelines.  Standard 4.63 provides for reprimand when "a lawyer negligently fails to provide a client with accurate or complete information, and causes actual or potential injury to the client."  Standard 7.3 provides for reprimand when "a lawyer negligently engages in conduct that is a violation of a duty owed to the profession and causes injury or potential injury to a client, the public, or the legal system."

Respondent violated his duty to his client, the public, and the legal profession by negligently failing to adequately communicate to his client the proposed change in his hourly fee schedule. As noted earlier, the injury caused by the litigation filed by Respondent against Mr. Rayburn included substantial adverse publicity that reflected poorly on the profession.  Any financial injury sustained by Rayburn as a result of Respondent's conduct was resolved and later settled to Mr. Rayburn's satisfaction.

Research into this matter has revealed only one case with a specific reference to a violation of Rule 1.5(b), In re Queyrouze, Board Docket No. 95-DB-040. In that case, ODC filed formal charges, consisting of two counts, against the attorney. Count I charged that the attorney failed to have a signed written contingency contract, failed to communicate the basis of the fee, failed to give an accounting for the fee, and charged an excessive and unreasonable fee. Count II charged that the attorney failed to make prompt and proper remittance of the funds owed to the client.

Following the filing of the formal charges, the parties filed a joint petition for consent discipline and the record was submitted for review. The hearing committee found that the attorney had presented a contingent fee contract to the client for signature, but that the client had failed to return an executed contract to the attorney. In spite of this, the attorney proceeded with the representation, ultimately settling the matter. The Board publicly reprimanded the attorney "for his oversight in failing to have his former client execute a contingent fee contract" in accordance with Rule 1.5(c), which states in part that a contingency fee contract shall be in writing.

While the instant matter can readily be distinguished from Queyrouze, the fact that the Board imposed a sanction of public reprimand in a matter involving multiple counts of alleged misconduct - including an alleged violation of Rule 1.5(b) - supports the conclusion that the misconduct in this matter does not warrant a more substantial discipline.  Therefore, the appropriate baseline sanction is public reprimand.

  The aggravating and mitigating factors noted by the Board do not justify an upward deviation from the proposed sanction of public reprimand.

RULING

Based on the foregoing, it is ordered that the petition for consent discipline be accepted, and that a public reprimand be issued against the respondent, Arthur A. Lemann III, for his violations of the Rules of Professional Conduct. It is further ordered that Respondent be assessed the costs and expenses of these proceedings, with legal interest to commence running thirty days from the date of finality of this ruling until paid.
