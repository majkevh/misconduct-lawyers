BURKS, JAMES E.
00-DB-125 (5/31/2001)Reprimand  Neglect of Legal Matters  

LOUISIANA ATTORNEY DISCIPLINARY BOARD

IN RE:JAMES E. BURKS

NUMBER 00-DB-125

RULING OF THE DISCIPLINARY BOARD

PER CURIAM

This is a disciplinary proceeding based on the filing of a Joint Petition for Consent Discipline by the Respondent, James E. Burks ("Respondent"), of Lake Charles, Louisiana, and the Office of Disciplinary Counsel ("ODC") prior to the filing of formal charges. The Board accepts the Petition and orders a public reprimand be issued against Respondent. 

PROCEDURAL HISTORY

On October 3, 2000, Respondent and ODC filed a Joint Petition for Consent Discipline. On the same day, the parties filed a stipulation of facts and memorandum in support of the proposed sanction. Formal charges have not been filed in this matter. On January 25, 2001, a panel of the Disciplinary Board heard oral arguments and reviewed the documents filed in this matter. Deputy Disciplinary Counsel James T. Daly represented ODC. James Spruel, Jr. appeared on behalf of the Respondent.

FACTUAL BACKGROUND 

The record, including the stipulation of facts, reveals the following information. Respondent was served with a complaint filed by Katherine L. Thomas on October 4, 1999.He failed to respond to the complaint in writing as required. A subpoena then was served upon him to obtain his sworn statement. He provided his statement and from thereon cooperated fully with the investigation of the matter. During the course of the investigation, Respondent completed the succession that was the source of the complaint. ODC states that it has received no further communication from the complainant since the completion of the succession. 

In another matter, Loretta Evans, a widow of Respondent's client, Willie Evans, filed a complaint. Respondent had accepted the representation of Mr. Evans in a medical malpractice case that had already been handled by two prior attorneys. Upon accepting representation, Respondent had the medical records reviewed by an expert witness. The resulting report did not support the malpractice claim. At this same time, Respondent experienced difficulty in getting a medical review panel set up to review the claim.[i] As a result, the case never went before the panel. Subsequently, the client died.

Respondent claims, but cannot prove, that he kept Mr. Evans informed about his case, that Mr. Evans understood the problems and accepted the outcome. Mrs. Evans filed the complaint approximately four years after the last action on the case. Respondent cooperated in the investigation of this complaint. 

PETITION FOR CONSENT DISCIPLINE

 In the joint petition, the parties submit that Respondent's misconduct in the above two instances constitutes violations of Rules of Professional Conduct 1.3 (failure to act with reasonable diligence), 1.4(a) (failure to keep a client reasonably informed and promptly comply with reasonable requests for information), 8.1(c) and 8.4(g)(failure to cooperate). Respondent tenders a conditional admission to the misconduct, and the parties propose that a sanction of public reprimand be imposed.

APPLICATION OF FACTORS CONSIDERED IN IMPOSING SANCTIONS

Respondent and ODC have agreed that Respondent has violated Rules 1.3, 1.4(a), 8.1(c), and 8.4(g) of the Rules of Professional Conduct. The Board must accept that Respondent has violated these rules. In re Lieberman, 95-2628 (La. 6/7/96); 675 So. 2d 272. Given the violations of these rules, the Board is left to determine if the proposed sanction of public reprimand is appropriate.

Louisiana Supreme Court Rule XIX, Section 10(C) states that in imposing a sanction after a finding of lawyer misconduct, the Court or Board shall consider the following factors: 

(1)whether the lawyer has violated a duty owed to a client, to the public, to the legal system, or to the profession;

 (2)whether the lawyer acted intentionally, knowingly, or negligently;

(3)the amount of actual or potential injury caused by the lawyer's misconduct; and 

(4)the existence of any aggravating or mitigating factors.

The record in this matter supports a finding that Respondent knowingly violated duties owed to his client and to the profession. However, the record fails to provide any solid proof of any harm to Respondent's clients. At most, harm to the clients here is minimal-Mr. Evans' case was unsupported by the medical evidence submitted to the expert, and Ms. Thomas' succession matter was ultimately completed.

Mitigating factors include absence of a dishonest motive, remorse, and a good faith effort to rectify the consequences of misconduct. Aggravating factors include prior disciplinary offenses [ii] and substantial experience in the practice of law [iii].

ANALYSIS

I. ABA Standards

 The Louisiana Supreme Court relies on the ABA's Standards for Imposing Lawyer Sanction (herein "Standard") to determine the baseline sanction by "the type of duty violated, the lawyer's mental state and the extent of the injury caused; and then adjust the sanction in accordance with the aggravating and mitigating factors present." In re Quaid, 94-1316 (La. 11/30/94); 646 So. 2d 343, 350.

The proposed sanction is in line with the ABA guidelines. Under A.B.A. Standard 4.43, a "reprimand is generally appropriate when a lawyer is negligent and does not act with reasonable diligence in representing a client, and causes injury or potential injury to the client. "Under Standard 7.3, a "reprimand is generally appropriate when a lawyer negligently engages in conduct that is a violation of a duty owed as a professional, and causes injury or potential injury to a client, the public, or the legal system."

II. Relevant Case Law

The Board also looks to relevant case law for assistance in determining an appropriate sanction for violations of the Rules of Professional Conduct. The case of In re Laiche, 98-DB-057, offers guidance here. In Laiche, formal charges were filed against the respondent consisting of five counts that alleged violations of Rules of Professional Conduct 1.3, 1.4(a)(b), 1.16(d), 8.1(c) and 8.4(g).After the respondent admitted to violating Rules 1.3, 1.4 and l.16 (d), the parties later filed a joint motion for consent discipline proposing a public reprimand as the appropriate discipline. Bd. Op. at 1.

Count I of the formal charges brought against Laiche alleged that the respondent filed suit on behalf of his clients in a personal injury matter but did little more to move the case forward over the next eight years, in violation of Rule 1.3.Count II of the charges alleged that Laiche failed to keep his clients informed as to the progress or lack of progress of the case, in violation of Rule 1.4(a).The other pertinent counts alleged that the respondent failed to timely inform his clients of a settlement offer, which he rejected without consulting his clients, and failed to return a file and to timely sign a motion to substitute counsel to clients who had discharged him.Id. at 1.-2. 

The only aggravating factor was respondent's substantial experience in the practice of law. Mitigating factors included the absence of a disciplinary record, no dishonest or selfish motive and a timely good faith effort to rectify the misconduct. Id. at 2.The Board accepted the joint petition, ordering that the respondent receive a public reprimand and that he be assessed with all costs and expenses of the proceedings. In reaching its determination, the Board emphasized the fact that the clients did not suffer any actual harm.Id. at 3. 

Another cases that lends guidance here is In re Brown, 00-DB-047.In that case, the respondent was issued a public reprimand for violations of Rules of Professional Conduct 1.3 and 1.4(a).There, the respondent received the file of a client from his former associate who had been handling the matter for several years. Although the client was notified in writing of the transfer of the file, the respondent did nothing substantive to move the matter forward or to keep the client informed as to the status of the case for over a year. The only aggravating factor present was the substantial experience in the practice of law. The absence of a prior disciplinary record was cited as a mitigating factor. Bd. Op. at 2-3.

ODC cites several cases in support of the consent discipline, including the case of In re Ruddick, 94-DB-060. In Ruddick, formal charges were filed against the respondent alleging violations of Rules of Professional Conduct 1.3, 1.4, 1.16 and 8.4(g).There, the respondent failed to provide his clients with a status reports regarding a succession matter, neglected to complete the succession and failed to communicate with his clients. He also failed to return documents to the clients and failed to remit an unearned fee. The respondent also failed to respond to inquiries from ODC concerning the clients' complaint. Prior to the hearing scheduled in the matter, the respondent filed a motion for consent discipline requesting that he receive a public reprimand for his misconduct. Bd. Op. at pp. 1-2.ODC concurred, and the Board approved the discipline. Mitigating factors included no prior disciplinary record and personal problems.Aggravating factors included multiple offenses and substantial experience in the practice of law.Id. at 3-4.

ODC also cites the case of In re Dolbear, 98-DB-061, in support of the sanction. In that case, the respondent was retained in a matter involving four imprisoned Mexican nationals and accepted money to obtain their release. He agreed to refund the money if he was unsuccessful. When he was unsuccessful, he failed to return the fee. In another matter, Dolbear agreed to review a file but failed to return it after the client decided not to retain him. He also failed to cooperate with ODC in its investigation of the complaints.

 The Board found that Dolbear negligently failed to act with reasonable diligence, negligently handled client property, and failed to cooperate with ODC. Numerous aggravating factors were cited by the Board, including prior misconduct, failure to cooperate, vulnerability of victim and substantial experience in the practice of law .The mitigating factor of absence of selfish motive was also noted. The respondent admitted that he violated Rules of Professional Conduct 1.3, 1.4, 1.5, 1.16(d), 8.1 and 8.4(a)(d)(g). The Board accepted the proposed consent discipline of a public reprimand .Bd. Op. at pp. 1-2.See also In re Taylor, 96-DB-053 (public reprimand issued for respondent's violation of Rules 1.3, 1.4, 8.4(a) and 8.4(d)).

III.Conclusion

This matter is akin to the above-cited cases in that similar rule violations are present. The aggravating factor of substantial experience in the practice of law is present in all instances, save the Taylor matter. The mitigating factor of absence of dishonest motive is present as in Laiche and Dolbear, and the mitigating factor of good faith effort to rectify the consequences of the misconduct is present as in Laiche.

Further, following the issuance of the subpoena in the Thomas matter, the Respondent did provide a sworn statement to ODC and from thereon cooperated fully with the investigation of the matter. Also, as previously noted, harm to the clients here is, at most, minimal.

RULING

Based on the foregoing, it is ordered that the Petition for Consent Discipline be accepted, and that a public reprimand be issued against the Respondent, James E. Burks. It is further ordered that Respondent be assessed the costs and expenses of these proceedings, with legal interest to commence running thirty days from the date of finality of this Ruling until paid.
