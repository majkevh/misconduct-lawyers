TOBIN, JAMES MICHAEL
99-DB-015 (10/12/2000) Reprimand Improper Conduct Before Judicial Tribunal

LOUISIANA ATTORNEY DISCIPLINARY BOARD

IN RE:  JAMES MICHAEL TOBIN

NUMBER 99-DB-015

RULING OF THE DISCIPLINARY BOARD

This is a disciplinary proceeding based on an admission of the formal charges filed by the Office of Disciplinary Counsel (herein "ODC").  The respondent is James Michael Tobin (herein "Respondent") formerly of New Orleans, Louisiana.

PROCEDURAL HISTORY

ODC filed formal charges, consisting of three counts, against Respondent on March 11, 1999.  On April 12, 1999, Respondent, pro se, answered the formal charges admitting the facts alleged but objecting to the severity of the violations ODC charged.  Respondent, now residing in Maryland, requested that this matter be resolved without a formal hearing, as he did not have the means to travel to Louisiana and conduct a defense.  On July 2, 1999, Hearing Committee Number 9 granted ODC's Motion to Submit Matter and the matter was ordered for submission on the documents on August 26, 1999.  The Respondent and ODC submitted no further documents.  Hearing Committee Number 9's report was due October 29, 1999.  The Chairman of Hearing Committee Number 9 was unable to complete the report and the matter was transferred to Hearing Committee Number 8 (herein the "Committee") on April 17, 2000.  The Committee submitted its report on May 3, 2000.  The Committee unanimously recommended that the Respondent receive an admonition for his conduct.

ODC objected to the Committee's recommendation of admonition and asserted that the Committee failed to take into account certain aggravating factors, such as Respondent's prior discipline record.  The Respondent did not file a response to ODC's objection or to the Committee's report.

A panel of the Board heard this matter June 29, 2000.  ODC was represented by James T. Daly and Respondent did not appear.

FORMAL CHARGES

The formal charges consist of three counts arising out of Respondent's failure to mail a copy of a pleading to a party pursuant to Respondent's endorsement of the certificate of service.   Respondent has admitted the allegations of the counts.

Count I:  Respondent failed to mail a pleading to a counsel of record despite having executed the certificate of service indicating that all counsel of record had been served by mail.  Despite a request by the Complainant for a copy of the document, Respondent still failed to forward a copy.  ODC avers that Respondent's conduct violates Rule 3.3(a)(1)[i], making a false statement to a tribunal, and Rule 8.4(d)[ii], conduct that is prejudicial to the administration of justice.

Count II:  Respondent failed to keep the Louisiana State Bar Association informed of his current address which delayed the service of the complaint for two years.  ODC asserts that Supreme Court Rule XIX, Section 8(c)[iii] has been violated.

Count III:  Respondent's combined actions constitute a violation of Rule 8.4(a)[iv], which is the violation or attempted violation of the Rules of Professional Conduct.

FINDINGS OF THE HEARING COMMITTEE

The Committee found that Respondent failed to serve one party in connection with his certificate of service attached to a pleading.  The Committee found that this was an isolated, negligent act and that there was no indication in the record of harm or injury due to Respondent's conduct.

Respondent has received two prior disciplinary sanctions[v].  The Committee rejected ODC's suggestion that the aggravating factors of "pattern of misconduct", "multiple offenses", "bad faith" and "substantial experience" under Standard 9.22(c), (d), (e) and (i) apply.  The Committee stated this was a single act of negligence by a practitioner whose practice of law spanned about only five years and whose prior discipline was unrelated to the instant matter.  Respondent has relocated to Maryland and it appeared to the Committee that he is no longer practicing law.  The Committee did find that the aggravating factor of prior disciplinary action under Standard 9.22(a) applied.  Neither ODC nor the Committee found any mitigating factors.

The Committee expressed its concern regarding a public reprimand as the appropriate sanction.  The Committee stated its concern that the disciplinary system "must be careful to avoid potential abuse of the system by disgruntled litigants; thus, this Committee views complaints lodged by opposing litigation counsel as almost presumptively suspect."  Hearing Committee Opinion No. 8, p. 4.  While the Committee underscored the importance of a tribunal's procedural rules, it noted that the parties could have more efficiently remedied the complaint of failure to mail a pleading.

The Committee recognized that Respondent does have obligations to forward pleadings and to update his address and that he does have a prior disciplinary record.  Because Respondent may seek to re-enter the practice of law, some degree of discipline is warranted.  The Committee recommended an admonition. 

OBJECTION TO THE HEARING COMMITTEE'S REPORT

ODC objects to the Committee's recommendation of admonition and argues that the Committee failed to give Respondent's prior discipline record sufficient weight as an aggravating factor.  ODC argues that once formal charges have been issued the sanction of admonition is no longer available.  Supreme Court Rule XIX, Section 10A(5).

According to ODC, both of Respondent's prior disciplinary actions involved his failure to cooperate in the process.  ODC argues that Respondent's failure to serve all counsel of record and failure to file his change of address ("even after being reminded by the Office of the Disciplinary Counsel") evidences Respondent's disregard for the Rules of Professional Conduct and the disciplinary system.  According to the ODC, the instant matter is a continuation of the Respondent's disregard for the disciplinary rules and process.  ODC asserts that a public reprimand is the baseline sanction and recommends suspension.

ANALYSIS

I.          THE STANDARD OF REVIEW

In as much as the Board is serving in an appellate capacity, the standard of review applied to findings of fact is that of "manifest error".  Arceneaux v. Domingue, 365 So.2d 1330 (La.1978);  Rosell v. Esco, 549 So.2d 840 (La. 1989).  The Board conducts a de novo review of the hearing committee's application of the Rules of Professional Conduct.  In re Hill, 90-DB-004.

II.         THE MANIFEST ERROR INQUIRY

ODC did not object to the facts found by the Committee.   The record supports the Committee's findings of fact.

III.       DE NOVO REVIEW OF THE APPLICATION OF THE RULES OF PROFESSIONAL CONDUCT

After reviewing the record in this matter and considering ODC's brief and oral argument, the Board finds the Respondent has violated the following rules:

1. Supreme Court Rule XIX, Section 8(c) has been violated.  A lawyer is required to submit a change of address to the Louisiana State Bar Association within thirty days of the change.  Respondent has admitted that he failed to timely change his address with the State Bar Association.

2. Rule 3.3(a)(1) has been violated.   The Rule sets forth that "a lawyer shall not knowingly:  (1) Make a false statement of material fact or law to a tribunal".  Respondent admitted in his letter dated August 12, 1998, and again on April 9, 1999, "that, if, in fact, I did fail to forward the pleading (whatever is [sic] was), it was an administrative oversight for which I apologized".  On its face, a certificate of service is a lawyer's pledge that he or she has complied with the service requirements.  Taken as a purely technical violation, Respondent violated the Rule.  However, ODC has not demonstrated that Respondent's failure to mail a pleading to one party in the case rises to the level of making a false statement of material fact to a court.  The record is bare of harm or prejudice to Complainant, Complainant's client, the court, or to any aspect of the case.

3. Rule 8.4(a) has been violated.  It is professional misconduct for a lawyer to violate or attempt to violate the Rules of Professional Conduct, knowingly assist or induce another to do so, or to do so through the acts of another.  Rule 3.3(a)(1) and Rule 8.4(d) have been violated which constitutes a violation of 8.4(a).

4. Rule 8.4(d) has been violated.   It is professional misconduct for a lawyer to engage in conduct that is prejudicial to the administration of justice.  Per his written responses dated August 12, 1998, and April 9, 1999, Respondent admitted and apologized for his failure to forward a copy of a pleading.  Respondent also admitted that he failed to keep the State Bar apprised of his current mailing address, which delayed the ODC's investigation by almost two years.  These two acts have caused prejudice to the administration of justice by slowing the process.

APPLICATION OF FACTORS TO BE CONSIDERED IN IMPOSING SANCTIONS

Louisiana Supreme Court Rule XIX, Section 10(C), states that in imposing a sanction after a finding of lawyer misconduct, the Court or Board shall consider the following factors:

(1) whether the lawyer has violated a duty owed to a client, to the public, to the legal system or to the profession;

(2) whether the lawyer acted intentionally, knowingly or negligently;

(3) the amount of actual or potential injury caused by the lawyer's misconduct; and

(4) the existence of any aggravating or mitigating factors.

The Board finds the Respondent has violated duties owed to the legal system and the profession by failing to keep his address current with the Bar, failing to abide by the certificate of service, violating the Rules of Professional Conduct and delaying the disciplinary investigation by an obsolete address.

The Louisiana Supreme Court has stated, " [a]n admonition cannot be imposed after formal charges have been issued".  Supreme Court Rules, Section 10A(5).  The Committee relied upon A.B.A. Standards for Imposing Lawyer Sanction Standards 6.14 and 6.24 in recommending that Respondent be admonished for his conduct.  Respondent has not objected to the Committee's findings.  ODC recommended Respondent, at the least, be publicly reprimanded.  In its pre-hearing memorandum, ODC suggested that suspension is the appropriate discipline.   ODC relied upon A.B.A. Standards for Imposing Lawyer Sanction standards 6.14 and 6.23.  Neither ODC, the Respondent nor the Committee cited any case law.

The penalty for violation of Supreme Court Rule XIX, Section 8C (the maintenance of a current registration statement) is "a notice of delinquency and imminent certification of ineligibility to practice law".  If an attorney fails to comply with the notice by August 31st, he is assessed a $25 fine and will be "summarily certified ineligible to practice law".   The record is void of information as to whether Respondent was assessed and/or paid the $25 fine and was certified ineligible to practice law.  Respondent now resides in Maryland and the Committee believes he no longer practices law.

Under A.B.A. Standards for Imposing Lawyer Sanction, Standard 6.14 applies to Rule 3.3(a)(1) which addresses duties owed as a professional.  Standard 6.14 states "[a]dmonition is generally appropriate when a lawyer engages in an isolated instance of neglect in determining whether submitted statements or documents are false or in failing to disclose material information upon learning of its falsity, and causes little or no actual or potential injury to a party, or causes little or no adverse or potentially adverse effect on the legal proceeding".  The Committee noted that the record is bare of harm or prejudice caused by Respondent's failure to mail a pleading.  In April 1996, Complainant states that he learned of the pleading from another counsel of record who received a copy of the pleading.  In Complainant's April 1997 letter to the Disciplinary Counsel, he states " Mr. Tobin has still never served me with copies of pleadings he has filed in this litigation as I complained of".  Complainant, while he may have been inconvenienced by having to obtain his own copy from the court or other counsel of record, never says that he, his client, the court or the case was harmed.      

Under A.B.A. Standards for Imposing Lawyer Sanction, Standards 6.23 and 6.24 apply to Rule 8.4(d) which applies to duties owed to the legal system.  Standard 6.23 provides "[r]eprimand is generally appropriate when a lawyer negligently fails to comply with a court order or rule, and causes injury or potential injury to a client or other party, or causes interference or potential interference with a legal proceeding".  Standard 6.24 permits an admonition for "an isolated instance of negligence in complying with a court order or rule, and causes little or no actual or potential injury to a party, or causes little or no actual or potential interference with a legal proceeding".

The Respondent has two prior discipline actions.  Respondent was admonished in August 1996, for failure to determine his client's objectives, lack of diligence in completing the work, failure to keep his client informed and failure to cooperate in the investigation of the complaint. 

In November 1996, Respondent was reprimanded primarily for his failure to respond to the charges and timely defend himself.  Initially, Respondent was charged with failing to complete the representation of a client, act with diligence, promptness or proper communication, provide an accounting and cooperate with the disciplinary investigation.  Despite faulty Bar Association records used in sending information to a wrong address, eventually, the Respondent did receive notice that his failure to respond would have the effect of the charges being deemed admitted.  Respondent did not respond until the briefing period and the Hearing Committee had already signed an order providing the counts were deemed admitted.  From the Respondent's brief, the Board noted the difficulty in representing the Complainant and commented that Respondent appeared to have more than earned the fee charged.  However, the Board recommended Respondent be reprimanded for his failure to timely defend himself of the charges.

ODC argues that the Committee has not given Respondent's prior history of disregard for the disciplinary system proper weight in determining sanctions in the instant matter.  Unlike the prior matters, Respondent did respond to the charges in the instant matter and admitted the underlying facts.  The Committee determined that Respondent's conduct in this matter is unrelated to the two prior proceedings and is not evidence of a pattern of misconduct.  The Committee refused to consider the prior discipline in reaching its recommended sanction in this matter.

A.B.A. Standard 8.4 provides "[a]n admonition is generally not an appropriate sanction when a lawyer violated the terms of a prior disciplinary order or when a lawyer has engaged in the same or similar misconduct in the past".   The comments indicate that an admonition is appropriate only when the act "causes little or no injury to a client, the public, the legal system or the profession and where the lawyer is unlikely to engage in further misconduct".  Respondent did not violate a prior disciplinary order or commit the same or similar misconduct.  As already mentioned, there is no evidence of harm caused to anyone.  Respondent is no longer practicing law in Louisiana, if at all.

A reprimand under Standard 8.3 is appropriate when the respondent violates a prior disciplinary order or has previously been admonished for the same or similar misconduct and continues to engage in similar acts that cause injury or potential injury to a client, the public, the legal system or the profession.  The Board finds the Respondent's conduct is an isolated, single event that is not related to his prior disciplinary proceedings.

Pursuant to the A.B.A. Standards for Imposing Lawyer Sanction, aggravating and mitigating factors may be taken into account when determining the sanction.  The Board finds the aggravating factors of Standards 9.22(c), (d), (e) and (i) do not apply.  Respondent's conduct does not reflect a "pattern of misconduct" or "multiple offenses" because this is a single act of negligence and Respondent's prior discipline is unrelated.  Furthermore, the Board does not find bad faith obstruction of the legal system or Supreme Court Rule XIX, Section 8(C) (the maintenance of a current registration statement).  Instead, the Board believes that Respondent left Louisiana and the practice of law and that his failure to update his address was an oversight that was not intended to violate court rules.  Respondent's five years of practice did not make him a "substantially experienced" attorney.  Like the Committee, the Board recognizes Standard 9.22(a), prior disciplinary offenses, as the only aggravating factor.  The Board finds no mitigating factors.

Under the A.B.A. Standards, a sanction of suspension in this matter is inappropriate but an admonition or reprimand is proper.  A reprimand is the minimum sanction permitted by the Louisiana Supreme Court given the fact that formal charges were issued.

RULING

After considering the Hearing Committee's Report, ODC's brief and oral argument and the record, the Disciplinary Board finds the Respondent violated Supreme Court Rule XIX, Section 8(c), and Rules of Professional Conduct 3.3(a)(1), 8.4(a) and (d).  The Disciplinary Board therefore orders the Respondent, James Michael Tobin, be issued a public reprimand and assessed all disciplinary costs associated with the disciplinary investigation and these disciplinary proceedings, with legal interest to commence running thirty days from the date of finality of the Ruling until paid.
