BOUDREAUX, MICHAEL K.
05-DB-049 (05/23/2006) Public Reprimand

LOUISIANA ATTORNEY DISCIPLINARY BOARD

IN RE:  MICHAEL K. BOUDREAUX

NUMBER:  05-DB-049

RULING OF THE DISCIPLINARY BOARD

This is a disciplinary proceeding based on the filing of formal charges against the Respondent, Michael K. Boudreaux, by the Office of Disciplinary Counsel ("ODC").  For reasons stated below, the Disciplinary Board orders that the Respondent receive a public reprimand and be assessed with all costs and expenses of these proceedings.

PROCEDURAL HISTORY

Formal charges, consisting of one count, were filed against the Respondent by ODC on May 11, 2005.[1]  In the charges, ODC alleges that the Respondent violated Rules of Professional Conduct 1.8(h), 8.4(a) and 8.4(c)[2] in connection with his representation of a client in a worker's compensation matter.  The charges were served upon the Respondent via certified mail on May 13, 2005.  The Respondent failed to answer the charges, and consequently, ODC filed a "Motion to Declare Factual Allegations Deemed Proven and Scheduling Written Arguments" on June 8, 2005. 

ODC's motion was granted by the chair of Hearing Committee Number 15, David Kimmel, on June 22, 2005.  In the order granting the motion, the factual allegations contained in the formal charges were deemed admitted and proven by clear and convincing evidence, the Respondent was given twenty days from the mailing of the order to demonstrate good cause why the imposition of the order would be improper, and the parties were given until August 17, 2005 to file written arguments and documentary evidence on the issue of sanctions.

The Respondent did not file a motion to recall the deemed admitted order.  ODC filed its "Deemed Admitted Submission on Sanctions" on August 17, 2005.  The Respondent did not file a submission.  The hearing committee issued its report on December 2, 2005, finding that the Respondent violated the Rules of Professional Conduct as charged and recommending that the Respondent receive a public reprimand.  On December 27, 2005, ODC filed a notice indicating that it had no opposition to the hearing committee's opinion.  On February 22, 2006, the Respondent faxed a letter to the Disciplinary Board indicating that he admitted the formal charges, that he had no objection to the hearing committee's report, and that he would not attend the oral argument before the Disciplinary Board.  Oral argument before Panel "B" of the Disciplinary Board was held on February 23, 2006.  Deputy Disciplinary Counsel Shana M. Broussard appeared on behalf of ODC. As previously indicated in his letter, the Respondent did not appear.

THE FORMAL CHARGES

The formal charges in this matter read, in pertinent part, as follows:

"In or about 1988, Ralph A. Steptoe hired BOUDREAUX to pursue a worker's compensation claim.  In April of 1999, BOUDREAUX met with Mr. Steptoe and his brother.  At that time, BOUDREAUX notified Mr. Steptoe in writing that the worker's compensation claim had prescribed.  On July 10, 2000 and February 22, 2002[3], BOUDREAUX prepared settlement sheets and forwarded payments to Mr. Steptoe in the amount of $10,000.00 for a total of $20,000.00.  The context of the settlement sheet provided:

This is to verify that I received $10,000.00 (ten thousand and no/100 dollars) on July 10, 2000 from Michael K. Boudreaux as partial settlement of my claim from my workers' compensation case.

/s/

Ralph Steptoe

BOUDREAUX attempted to settle a claim for malpractice by the payment of $20,000.00.  BOUDREAUX failed to advise Mr. Steptoe in writing that independent representation was appropriate.

BOUDREAUX's conduct violated Rules 1.8(h), 8.4(a) and 8.4(c) of the Rules of Professional Conduct."

THE HEARING COMMITTEE'S REPORT

As stated above, the hearing committee issued its report on December 2, 2005.  In its report, the committee found that the factual allegations of the formal charges had been deemed admitted and proven by clear and convincing evidence.  The committee also determined that Rules 1.8(h), 8.4(a), and 8.4(c) had been violated by the Respondent, and it  relied upon ABA Standards for Imposing Lawyer Sanctions, Standard 4.33[4], in deciding that the baseline sanction was a public reprimand.  The committee found that the aggravating factors of dishonest or selfish motive, substantial experience in the practice of law[5], and prior disciplinary record[6]  were present.  The committee further determined that the mitigating factors of substantial restitution in the amount of $20,000.00 and timely good faith effort to make restitution or to rectify the consequences of the misconduct were present.  The committee concluded that a public reprimand was the appropriate sanction. 

ODC filed a notice indicating that it had no opposition to the hearing committee's report on December 27, 2005.  In his letter of February 22, 2006 faxed to the Disciplinary Board, the Respondent also indicated that he had no objection to the hearing committee's report.

ANALYSIS

I.  The Standard Of Review

The powers and duties of the Disciplinary Board are defined in Section 2 of the Louisiana Supreme Court Rule XIX, Rules for Lawyer Disciplinary Enforcement.  Subsection (G)(2)(a) states that the Board is "to perform appellate review functions, consisting of review of the findings of fact, conclusions of law, and recommendations of hearing committees with respect to formal charges . . . and prepare and forward to the court its own findings, if any, and recommendations."  Inasmuch as the Board is serving in an appellate capacity, the standard of review applied to findings of fact is that of "manifest error."  Arceneaux v. Domingue, 365 So. 2d 1330 (La.1978); Rosell v. ESCO, 549 So. 2d 840 (La. 1989).  The Board conducts a de novo review of the hearing committee's application of the Rules of Professional Conduct.  In re Hill, 90-DB-004. Here, the factual findings[7] of the hearing committee in this deemed admitted matter are supported by the factual allegations asserted by ODC in the formal charges and/or by the evidence submitted in support of the allegations, save the allegation that Respondent allowed Mr. Steptoe's case to "prescribe."  See In re Donnan, 01-3058 (La. 1/10/03); 838 So.2d 715.  The evidence submitted in support of the allegations shows that suit was timely filed on behalf of Mr. Steptoe, but that the Respondent allowed the case to become abandoned.  See La. Code Civ. Proc. Ann. art. 561 (West 2006); ODC Exhibit 10, Sworn Statement of Respondent, pp. 5-6.[8]  Nevertheless, based upon its other factual findings, the committee properly found that the Respondent violated Rules 1.8(h), 8.4(a) and 8.4(c) of the Rules of Professional Conduct, and, as discussed below, the Board adopts these findings. 

At the time of the misconduct in this matter, the applicable version of Rule 1.8(h) provided that a lawyer could not settle a claim for malpractice liability with an unrepresented client or former client without first advising that person in writing that independent representation was appropriate.  Here, at his April 1999 meeting with Mr. Steptoe, Respondent told Mr. Steptoe that he owed him money because he had allowed his case to "prescribe."  He also advised Mr. Steptoe of his option of seeking independent counsel to file a malpractice suit against him. Respondent did not put this information into writing, however.  At his sworn statement, Respondent testified as follows:

"I don't know why I didn't put that [the information concerning Mr. Steptoe's legal options] in writing.  I just assumed that the conversation, with his brother there making the interpretation, that—that should have conveyed the information to him.  I did follow-up with a letter, which I gave to him, and I think I also mailed, I don't know why I would have done it if I hadn't mailed it, advising him and thanking him for meeting with me and confirming that, you know, I had described that his case had prescribed, and I think I—I have given you a copy of – of that letter."[9]

ODC Exhibit 10, Sworn Statement of Respondent, p. 11.

In response to ODC's question as to whether settlement of Mr. Steptoe's claim was discussed at the meeting, Respondent continued:

"Yes. . . . [W]hat I told him at that meeting was that, you know, 'Mr. Steptoe, I owe you money for your case.  It's my fault that the case prescribed and, at some point, I will have to pay you money for your case, whether you go to an attorney or don't go to an attorney, whether you go to the Disciplinary Board or don't go to the Disciplinary Board, it's my responsibility to try to compensate you for that.'"

Id. at p. 12.

Respondent also testified at his sworn statement that at the meeting, no specific settlement amount was discussed.  Id. at 11-12.  However, in February of 2000, he sent Mr. Steptoe the first partial payment of $10,000.  He sent Mr. Steptoe another payment of $10,000 in July of 2000.  Mr. Steptoe cashed both checks. Id. at  27.  ODC does not contest the fact that Respondent orally advised his client to seek independent counsel prior to settling the case.  Nevertheless, a Rule 1.8(h) violation is present as Respondent failed to put this advice in writing.

Further, a Rule 8.4(c) violation also is present.  At the time of the misconduct at hand, this rule provided that it is professional misconduct for a lawyer to engage in conduct involving dishonesty, fraud, deceit or misrepresentation.   ODC takes issue with the two "settlement sheets" Respondent sent with the two $10,000.00 payments to Mr. Steptoe.  ODC specifically maintains that that "Respondent forwarded two 'settlement sheets' and checks in the amount of $10,000 under the guise of settling a worker's compensation case.  The client could only believe with the misrepresentation of the respondent that his worker's compensation claim had settled."  These facts, as restated in the formal charges, along with Mr. Steptoe's complaint and letters to ODC in which he questions the whereabouts of additional settlement funds[10], properly form the basis of a Rule 8.4(c) violation.  After reading the settlement sheets, Mr. Steptoe was apparently misled into believing that the worker's compensation claim had been settled and that he had additional settlement funds due from the worker's compensation insurer.

Because violations of Rules 1.8(h) and 8.4(c) are present, a violation of Rule 8.4(a) must also be found.  The applicable version of this rule provided that it was professional misconduct for a lawyer to "violate . . .  the Rules of Professional Conduct . . .". 

II.  The Appropriate Sanction

A.  Application of the Rule XIX, Section 10(C) Factors

Louisiana Supreme Court Rule XIX, Section 10(C) states that in imposing a sanction after a finding of lawyer misconduct, the Court or Board shall consider the following factors:

(1) whether the lawyer has violated a duty owed to a client, to the public, to the legal system, or to the profession;

(2) whether the lawyer acted intentionally, knowingly, or negligently;

(3) the amount of actual or potential injury caused by the lawyer's misconduct; and

(4) the existence of any aggravating or mitigating factors.

Here, the Respondent has violated duties owed to his client, Mr. Steptoe.  His conduct of settling the malpractice claim without advising Mr. Steptoe in writing to seek independent counsel appears to be negligent.  In his sworn statement, Respondent states that he explained to Mr. Steptoe in a meeting that his claim had "prescribed" and that he owed Mr. Steptoe compensation for his case.  He further explained to Mr. Steptoe that he had the option of filing a disciplinary complaint against him and/or of hiring another attorney to review his case and to bring a malpractice suit against him.  He negligently failed, however, to place this information in writing. 

Potential injury was present in that Mr. Steptoe is hearing impaired and mute, and he could have easily been taken advantage of without this information being placed in writing.  The record shows, however, that the Respondent did not do so.  As noted above, the Respondent advised Mr. Steptoe in writing that his claim had "prescribed."  See ODC Exhibit 11.  The settlement amount paid to Mr. Steptoe was reasonable and fair. See Hrg. Comm. Rpt., p. 4.[11]

The aggravating factors of substantial experience in the practice of law, dishonest or selfish motive and prior disciplinary offense are present.  The mitigating factor of timely good faith effort to make restitution or to rectify consequences of misconduct is also present.

B.  Louisiana Case Law

The hearing committee noted in its report that it reviewed the cases of In re Fazande, 2003-2210 (La. 1/21/04), 864 So.2d 174 and In re Dunn, 1998-0535 (La. 6/5/98), 713 So.2d 461 in reaching its proposed sanction of a public reprimand.  In Fazande, two counts of formal charges were filed against the respondent.  The first count alleged that the respondent neglected a legal matter and allowed the case to be dismissed upon motion of the opposing party. The second count alleged that he allowed another claim to prescribe and then failed to advise his clients in writing to seek outside counsel to discuss their potential malpractice claims.  Numerous aggravating factors were present, including prior discipline[12], substantial legal experience, pattern of misconduct, and multiple offenses.  The mitigating factor of cooperative attitude towards the proceedings and absence of a dishonest or selfish motive were also present.  Id. at 178-79.  The Court held that the respondent did not violate Rule 1.8(h) of the Rules of Professional Conduct because the settlement discussions never progressed to the point of a formal offer of settlement by the respondent or an acceptance of any offer by his clients.  Id. at 180.  Consequently, the Court imposed a six month suspension, with three months deferred, followed by a one year period of probation, based upon respondent's lack of diligence and failure to communicate with his clients in both counts.  Id. at 180-81.

In Dunn, the respondent was retained by a client to pursue a personal injury case arising out of a vehicular accident.  While respondent timely filed suit on the client's behalf, he failed to actively pursue the case for over five years, ultimately resulting in a dismissal of the suit on grounds of abandonment.  After the client advised respondent of the dismissal of her lawsuit, he acknowledged his error and offered to settle with her for the amount of $4,500.00.  However, he did not advise the client to seek advice from independent counsel on the settlement. Shortly thereafter, the respondent forwarded a check to his client in the amount of $1,500.00. Id. at 462. The Board noted that numerous aggravating factors were present, including substantial experience in the practice of law, prior discipline[13], selfish motive, pattern of misconduct and vulnerability of victim.  Mitigating factors included respondent's personal problems, acknowledgement of his error, his immediate attempt to make his client whole and candor with his client.  Id. at 463.  After reviewing the matter, the Court noted that while the respondent had clearly violated the Rules of Professional Conduct, the record supported the hearing committee's finding that "respondent intended to be fair and reasonable and he was not attempting to exploit his representation of the client."  Under such circumstances, the Court concluded that the respondent be suspended from the practice of law for a period of six months, all deferred, subject to a one year probationary period with conditions.  Id. at 464.

Finally, the case of In re Thompson, 1998-0079 (La. 5/8/98), 712 So.2d 72 also offers guidance here.  In Thompson, the respondent was retained by a client to pursue a worker's compensation claim.  The respondent failed to timely pursue the claim and believed it had prescribed.  Since respondent did not have professional liability insurance, he made a "personal agreement" with the client to advance up to $7,500.00 to her.  After taking into account his fee, the respondent paid the client approximately $5,000.00 over a two year period and had his client execute a "distribution statement" drafted in the language of tort claim receipt and release agreement.  Id. at 73.  The parties stipulated that the mitigating factors of no prior disciplinary record, free disclosure with ODC and inexperience in the practice of law were present.  The aggravating factors of dishonest or selfish motive, vulnerability of victim and refusal to acknowledge the wrongful conduct were adopted by the Court.  Id. at 73-74.  The Court also adopted the hearing committee's finding that the respondent permitted his client's case to prescribe and later became involved in a conflict of interest when he tried to settle his liability without advising his client to seek independent legal advice.  Id. at 74.  The Court imposed a suspension of one year, all deferred, subject to an eighteen month period of supervised probation with conditions.  Id.

The matter at hand is similar to Dunn and Thompson in that the Respondent did not follow the guidelines of Rule 1.8(h) in attempting to settle his malpractice matter with his client.  Unlike Dunn and Thompson, Mr. Boudreaux did advise his client to seek independent counsel.  Nevertheless, he failed to place this advice in writing.  Further, like the respondent in Dunn, Mr. Boudreaux actions appear to be "fair and reasonable" and "not an attempt to exploit his representation of the client."  Because of the negligent nature of Respondent's conduct and his good faith efforts to make restitution to Mr. Steptoe, a public reprimand as recommended by ODC and the hearing committee, instead of a deferred suspension, appears to be appropriate.

RULING

Based on the above, the Board modifies the hearing committee's report to correct the finding that the Respondent allowed Mr. Steptoe's case to prescribe; instead, the Board finds that the case was abandoned.  The Board adopts the hearing committee's report in all other respects and orders that the Respondent receive a public reprimand. 

The Board also orders that the Respondent be assessed with all costs and expenses of these proceedings.
