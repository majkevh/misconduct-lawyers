VEGA, JOSE W.
01-DB-024 (06/04/2001) Reprimand Neglect of Legal Matters

LOUISIANA ATTORNEY DISCIPLINARY BOARD

IN RE:  JOSE W. VEGA

NUMBER 01-DB-024

RULING OF THE DISCIPLINARY BOARD

PER CURIAM

This is a disciplinary proceeding based on the filing of a joint petition for consent discipline, prior to the institution of formal charges, by Jose W. Vega ("Respondent") of Houston, Texas, an attorney licensed to practice law in the State of Louisiana, and the Office of Disciplinary Counsel ("ODC"). For reasons stated below, the Disciplinary Board accepts the petition and orders a public reprimand be issued against Respondent.

PROCEDURAL HISTORY

On February 14, 2001, ODC and Respondent filed a joint petition for consent discipline and stipulation of facts in this matter.  On March 26, 2001, Respondent filed an initial brief in support of the proposed sanction. No formal charges have been filed in this matter.

On April 26, 2001, a panel of the Disciplinary Board reviewed this matter. Deputy Disciplinary Counsel G. Fred Ours represented ODC. Respondent appeared, pro se.

STIPULATED FACTS

The stipulation of facts filed in this matter provides as follows:

Miras Dusevic retained Mr. Vega on September 30, 1996 to represent him in his deportation proceedings at the Immigration Court in Oakdale, Louisiana.  Mr. Vega made several court appearances on behalf of Mr. Dusevic in connection with an application for relief from deportation.  Mr. Vega also represented Mr. Dusevic in a bond hearing, and Mr. Dusevic was able to post bond and was released from custody from the Immigration and Naturalization Service ("INS").  On May 14, 1997, the INS placed additional charges of deportability on Mr. Dusevic as a result in the change of law.  The new charges rendered Mr. Dusevic ineligible from relief from deportation, and he was ordered deported by the Immigration Judge.  After Mr. Dusevic was ordered deported, Mr. Vega charged him $1,500.00 to file an appeal.  Mr. Dusevic sent Mr. Vega the $1,500.00 after the appeal was due.  Mr. Vega agreed to file a late appeal for Mr. Dusevic; however, he did not file a late appeal because the deportation officer for the INS was requiring that Mr. Dusevic surrender himself into custody if he wanted to file a late appeal.  Mr. Dusevic agreed to self-deportation so he would not have to return to the custody of the INS.

Mr. Vega committed an ethical violation by stating to Mr. Dusevic that he would file a late appeal for him, accepting a $1,500.00 fee to do so, and not filing the late appeal on Mr. Dusevic's behalf and immediately returning the $1,500.00. His conduct violated Rules of Professional Conduct 1.3 and 1.16(d).

Mr. Vega thought that it was in his client's best interest not to file a late appeal because the INS stated to him that Mr. Dusevic would have to surrender himself into custody if he wanted to continue fighting the case.  Mr. Vega was able to negotiate an indefinite surrender date and a self-deportation for his client.  However, Mr. Vega obviously did not clearly explain to Mr. Dusevic the conditions of those terms.

Mr. Vega has now returned the $1,500.00 that he was paid for the late appeal.  Mr. Dusevic took all of the original documents from his file and he did not return these documents to Mr. Vega.  Mr. Dusevic has been voluntarily deported.

Mr. Vega has taken remedial steps to assure that this situation does not arise again.  He now prepares a detailed client retainer agreement before he begins working on a case.  He keeps a detailed time chart for each file.  He records a summary of every conversation that he has with a client.  He clearly explains to each client the options that exist prior to being retained, and he does not make important case strategy decisions without the clear consent of the client.  Please note that he has gradually implemented these procedures and has not received any complaints at all in almost two years, even though his practice has increased throughout this period of time.

The errors that Mr. Vega committed in this matter occurred within eighteen months of beginning his solo practice.  He has not repeated those errors, and has taken steps within his practice to assure that these errors are never repeated.

II.

Pedro Estevez retained Mr. Vega in December of 1995 to represent him in his deportation proceedings in Oakdale, Louisiana, for a fee of $1,500.00. Mr. Estevez, through his attorney Mr. Vega, applied for relief from deportation, which was denied by the Immigration Judge, and Mr. Vega filed a timely appeal of the Judge's decision.  While on appeal, there was a change in the law which allowed Mr. Estevez to reopen his proceeding with the Immigration Judge.  Mr. Vega was paid $1,000.00 to reopen the proceedings for Mr. Estevez, and to obtain a bond for him.  Mr. Vega submitted a motion to reopen to the Board of Immigration Appeals in May of 1997, and the Board of Immigration Appeals reopened the case on May 19, 1997.  On June 18, 1997, the Immigration Judge ordered Mr. Estevez removed from the United States for a second time.  Mr. Vega reserved Mr. Estevez's right to appeal by July 18, 1997.  On June 30, 1997, Mr. Vega represented Mr. Estevez in a bond hearing, and the Immigration Judge set a bond in the case.  Mr. Vega did not file a motion to withdraw as the attorney of record after Mr. Estevez bonded out.  After the time had expired to file an appeal, Mr. Vega realized that he was still attorney of record, and he attempted to file a late appeal. Shortly thereafter, Mr. Estevez's new attorney enrolled as the attorney of record.  Mr. Estevez was subsequently deported.

Mr. Vega admits that he did not properly and timely withdraw as the attorney of record in this matter.  Upon realizing that no appeal had been filed, Mr. Vega attempted to file a late appeal because he was still the attorney of record.  Mr. Vega was the attorney of record, and he should have either withdrawn as the attorney of record or filed a timely appeal.  His conduct violated Rules of Professional Conduct 1.3 and 1. 16. 

He has implemented measures in his office practice to insure that this situation does not occur again.  He has implemented a tickler system, and also records in the file all of the conversations that he has with his clients.  Mr. Vega has not had any similar problems for more than two years.  This incident occurred one year after he became a solo practitioner (July 1997), and he has taken the necessary means to assure that it does not happen again.

PETITION FOR CONSENT DISCIPLINE

In the petition, Respondent admits to violating Rule 1.3 (lack of diligence) and Rule 1.16 (improper termination of representation) of the Rules of Professional Conduct. The parties propose that a formal public reprimand be issued to Respondent.

APPLICATION OF FACTORS CONSIDERED IN IMPOSING SANCTIONS

Respondent and ODC have agreed that Respondent has violated Rules 1.3 and 1.16 of the Rules of Professional Conduct.  The Board must accept that Respondent has violated these rules.  In re Lieberman, 95-2628 (La. 6/7/96); 675 So. 2d 272. Given the violations of these rules, the Board is left to determine if the proposed sanction is appropriate.

Louisiana Supreme Court Rule XIX, Section 10(C) states that in imposing a sanction after a finding of lawyer misconduct, the Court or Board shall consider the following factors:

1. whether the lawyer has violated a duty owed to a client, to the public, to the legal system, or to the profession;

2. whether the lawyer acted intentionally, knowingly, or negligently;

3. the amount of actual or potential injury caused by the lawyer's misconduct; and

4. the existence of any aggravating or mitigating factors.

Based upon a review of the record, the Board finds that Respondent has negligently violated duties owed to his clients. The parties contend that Respondent's misconduct in this matter caused very little actual injury to Respondent's clients. The record supports this contention. While Respondent failed to timely file appeals on behalf of his clients, ultimately both clients were deported: one voluntarily and one by legal proceeding. Additionally, Respondent's failure to timely return the fee received from Mr. Dusevic did deprive his client of those funds for a limited time. However, the fee has now been returned to Mr. Dusevic.

The mitigating factors of absence of a prior disciplinary record, absence of a dishonest or selfish motive, lack of experience, cooperative attitude toward these proceedings, and remorse are present here. The sole aggravating factor present in this matter is multiple offenses.

Finally, the proposed sanction is in keeping with the ABA Standards[i] and prior jurisprudence.[ii]

RECOMMENDATION

Based on the foregoing, it is ordered that the petition for consent discipline be accepted, and that a public reprimand be issued against the respondent, Jose W. Vega. The Board also orders the Respondent be assessed all costs and expenses of these proceedings, with legal interest to commence running thirty days from the date of this order until paid.
