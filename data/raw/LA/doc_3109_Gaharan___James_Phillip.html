01-DB-046 (09/05/2001) Reprimand  Neglect of Legal Matters 

LOUISIANA ATTORNEY DISCIPLINARY BOARD

IN RE: JAMES P. GAHARAN, JR.

NUMBER 01-DB-046

RULING OF THE DISCIPLINARY BOARD

PER CURIAM

This is a disciplinary proceeding based on the filing of a joint petition for consent discipline by the Office of Disciplinary Counsel ("ODC") and James P. Gaharan, Jr. ("Respondent") of Lake Charles, Louisiana, an attorney licensed to practice law in the State of Louisiana. For reasons stated below, the Disciplinary Board accepts the petition and orders a public reprimand be issued against Respondent.

PROCEDURAL HISTORY

On May 7, 2001, ODC and Respondent filed a joint petition for consent discipline and stipulation of facts in this matter. On that same day, ODC filed a memorandum in support of the proposed sanction. No formal charges have been filed in this matter.

On June 28, 2001, a panel of the Disciplinary Board reviewed this matter. The parties waived appearances; therefore, oral argument was not conducted.

STIPULATED FACTS

The stipulated facts in this matter provide the following information:

Respondent performed a real estate closing on June 18, 1999, in which Brenda Shelton was the seller of the property. Respondent received the funds to pay off a loan of $70,523.55 owed by Ms. Shelton to Resource Bancshares Mortgage Group, Inc. ("RBMG"). However, Ms. Shelton later discovered that RBMG had not been paid.

After being informed that RBMG had not been paid, Respondent sent a check to RBMG. This check was returned marked "NSF" on August 16, 1999. Respondent explained that this problem arose when he prematurely disbursed funds in another closing. Eventually, RBMG received the full payment from Respondent.[i]

On a second occasion, on August 31, 1999, Respondent handled another real estate in which Frances Fisher was the seller of the property. At the time of the closing, Respondent withheld the cost of updating the title abstract and termite inspection from the seller's proceeds. Respondent thereafter failed to timely pay these costs, despite repeated demands from the seller's real estate agent. The title abstract company was finally paid on January 19, 2000, and the pest control company was paid on February 23, 2000.[ii]

PETITION FOR CONSENT DISCIPLINE

In the petition, Respondent admits to violating Rule 1.15[iii] and Rule 8.4(a)[iv] of the Rules of Professional Conduct. The parties propose that a formal public reprimand be issued to Respondent and that Respondent be placed on probation for a two-year period, with the following terms and conditions:

1)  A probation monitor will be appointed for the two-year period;

2)  Respondent will be monitored by a certified public accountant as part of the probation;

3)  The certified public accountant shall conduct quarterly audits of Respondent's client trust account during the probationary period and shall report the findings of such audits to the probation monitor; and

4)  Respondent shall pay all costs associated with these audits.

APPLICATION OF FACTORS CONSIDERED IN IMPOSING SANCTIONS

Respondent and ODC have agreed that Respondent has violated Rules 1.15 and 8.4(a) of the Rules of Professional Conduct.  The Board must accept that Respondent has violated these rules.  In re Lieberman, 95-2628 (La. 6/7/96); 675 So. 2d 272. Given the violations of these rules, the Board is left to determine if the proposed sanction is appropriate.

Louisiana Supreme Court Rule XIX, Section 10(C) states that in imposing a sanction after a finding of lawyer misconduct, the Court or Board shall consider the following factors:

(1) whether the lawyer has violated a duty owed to a client, to the public, to the legal system, or to the profession;

(2) whether the lawyer acted intentionally, knowingly, or negligently;

(3) the amount of actual or potential injury caused by the lawyer's misconduct; and

(4) the existence of any aggravating or mitigating factors.

Based upon a review of the record, the Board finds that Respondent has negligently violated duties owed to his clients. As a result, Respondent's clients were indirectly harmed by his actions, and third parties were directly harmed by being deprived of the funds due them for several months.

The mitigating factors of absence of a dishonest or selfish motive, timely good faith effort to rectify consequences of the misconduct, and full and free disclosure to the disciplinary board or cooperative attitude toward these proceedings are present here. The aggravating factors of a prior disciplinary record[v] and multiple offenses are also present.

Finally, the proposed sanction is in keeping with the ABA Standards[vi] and prior jurisprudence.[vii]

RULING

Based on the foregoing, it is ordered that the joint petition for consent discipline be accepted, and that a public reprimand be issued against the Respondent, James P. Gaharan, Jr., and that he be placed on probation for a two-year period with the following terms and conditions:

1)  A probation monitor will be appointed for the two-year period;

2)  Respondent will be monitored by a certified public accountant as part of the probation;

3)  The certified public accountant shall conduct quarterly audits of Respondent's client trust account during the probationary period and shall report the findings of such audits to the probation monitor; and

4)  Respondent shall pay all costs associated with these audits.

The Board also orders that all costs and expenses of these proceedings be assessed to Respondent, with legal interest to commence running thirty days from the date of this order until paid.

[i] In addition to the funds held in escrow to pay RBMG, Respondent also sent the necessary additional funds to pay interest which had accrued on the account from the date of the closing through actual receipt of the funds by RBMG on August 24, 1999.

[iii] Rule 1.15 is entitled "Safekeeping Property" and provides in pertinent part:

"(b) Upon receiving funds or other property in which a client or third person has an interest, a lawyer shall promptly notify the client or third person. Except as stated in this rule or otherwise permitted by law or by agreement with the client, a lawyer shall promptly deliver to the client or third person any funds or other property that the client or third person is entitled to receive and, upon request by the client or third person, shall promptly render a full accounting regarding such property."

[iv] Rule 8.4 is entitled "Misconduct" and provides in pertinent part:

"It is professional misconduct for a lawyer to:

"(a) Violate or attempt to violate the Rules of Professional Conduct, knowingly assist or induce another to do so, or do so through the acts of another; . . ."

[v] On January 28, 1999, Respondent was issued an admonition for violation of Rules 8.1(b), 8.1(c), and 8.4(g) of the Rules of Professional Conduct.

[vi] Standard 4.13 provides for reprimand "when a lawyer is negligent in dealing with client property and causes injury or potential injury to a client."

[vii] See In re Browning, Board Docket No. 98-DB-070 (La. 6/3/99) (Disciplinary Board approved public reprimand for attorney who failed to pay a third-party medical provider and failed to initially cooperate in the disciplinary investigation); In re Ford-Jones, Board Docket No. 99-DB-002 (La. 4/12/99) (Disciplinary Board approved public reprimand for attorney who failed to notify and deliver funds claimed by a medical provider); and In re Jones, Board Docket No. 93-DB-014 (La. 7/30/93)(Disciplinary Board approved public reprimand for attorney who commingled funds of a client with his own funds, failed to promptly disburse entrusted funds, and failed to properly supervise a non-lawyer employee).

DISSENT

Jack O. Whitehead, Jr.

As per the stipulation of facts, there were TWO cases of failure to pay a third party provider.In the first one, the Respondent's client forced the issue of non-payment and the matter was not settled until two months AFTER the Respondent missed his commitment to pay off a loan. Additionally, when the Respondent did send the check to the third party provider IT WAS RETURNED NSF.Regardless of Respondent's explanation to make this fact go away, this must be considered as either conversion or commingling of client funds.

In the second case, the Respondent failed to pay two third party providers - despite REPEATED DEMANDS from his client's agent. From the time Respondent withheld funds to pay until both providers were paid, nearly six months had elapsed.

Prior jurisprudence cited to justify a public reprimand dates as far back as 1993.At the minimum, the Respondent here was negligent and the behavior was knowing and/or intentional.After a real estate closing, Respondent had $70,523.55 in his possession to pay off his client's loan. He did not do so, until forced by his client.The check written and processed by the Respondent was returned NSF by the bank. From a factual standpoint, how can this NOT BE a conversion of commingling of client funds? It cannot be excused, nor does it fit in any other professional context.

I believe a public reprimand in this case is clearly TOO LENIENT, and therefore, DISSENT.

Jack O. Whitehead, Jr.
