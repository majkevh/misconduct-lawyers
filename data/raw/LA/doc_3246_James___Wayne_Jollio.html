JAMES, WAYNE JOLLIO
00-DB-147 (05/31/2001 ) Reprimand Probation  Conflict of Interest Neglect of Legal Matters

LOUISIANA ATTORNEY DISCIPLINARY BOARD

IN RE:  WAYNE JOLLIO JAMES

NUMBER 00-DB-147

RULING OF THE DISCIPLINARY BOARD

PER CURIAM

 This is a disciplinary proceeding based on the filing of an Amended Petition for Consent Discipline by the Respondent, Wayne Jollio James ("Respondent"), of New Orleans, Louisiana, prior to the filing of formal charges.  The Board accepts the Amended Petition and orders that a public reprimand be issued against Respondent.

PROCEDURAL HISTORY

On November 16, 2000, Respondent filed a Petition for Consent Discipline and Stipulation of Facts. On the same day, the Office of Disciplinary Counsel ("ODC") filed a Memorandum in Support of the Sanction and a Concurrence in the Consent Discipline.  On February 21, 2001, the Respondent filed an Amended Petition for Consent Discipline, changing a condition of probation previously proposed in his original Petition for Consent Discipline.  ODC filed an Amended Concurrence in Consent Discipline on February 21, 2001.  Formal charges have not been filed in this matter.  On February 22, 2001, a panel of the Disciplinary Board reviewed the documents filed in this matter. Both Deputy Disciplinary Counsel Eric K. Barefield and the Respondent waived their appearance before the Board and no oral argument was held. 

FACTUAL ALLEGATIONS

The record, including the stipulation of facts, reveals the following information.  In April of 1998, the Respondent was retained by Emeldia D. Wilson to handle her wrongful termination and employment discrimination case against her former employer.  Ms. Wilson maintained that she was fired because of racial discrimination.  Initially, Ms. Wilson had handled the case pro se. Respondent at first declined handling the case, because on its face it appeared to have various problems.  Though reluctant to accept the case, Respondent eventually enrolled as counsel of record on May 7, 1999.

   Respondent failed to diligently investigate and move the matter forward.  He did not conduct any formal discovery on Ms. Wilson's behalf and failed to answer discovery propounded by the defendants.  Additionally, he failed to oppose the defendant's motion to dismiss the complaint or motion for summary judgment.  Eventually, the court granted the motion for summary judgment.  Respondent also states in his stipulation of facts that "when a motion to compel was filed against [him] for sanctions, it created a conflict between himself and his client as to whether or not to continue pursuing the case." [i]

Respondent further states in his stipulation of facts that difficulties with the case and problems with his client negatively impacted his work in the matter.  He learned through defense counsel that Ms. Wilson's employment record included negative evaluations and multiple violations of company policy.  Due to conflicts between his client's version and the company's version of events, he was reluctant to move forward.  Ms. Wilson did not inform Respondent about the problems she had at work before hiring him to represent her.

  In mitigation, Respondent asserts that he should not have accepted the case, and when conflicts arose between his client and him he should have withdrawn.  He submits that on numerous occasions he urged Ms. Wilson to retain new counsel.  Further, he states that he has taken steps to improve his practice, including limiting his practice, purchasing a law office management program, and updating his tickler system.

Respondent was admitted to the practice of law in Louisiana in 1992.  He has been and is currently a sole practitioner.  He has no prior disciplinary record and is eligible to practice law. 

  PETITION FOR CONSENT DISCIPLINE

In the petition, Respondent admits that his conduct violated Rules of Professional Conduct 1.3,[ii] 1.7[iii] and 3.2.[iv] Respondent tenders a conditional admission to the misconduct, and the parties propose that the following sanction be imposed: 

A.  Public Reprimand with twelve (12) months probation. 

B. Respondent shall establish and maintain an effective calendaring system and method to communicate with clients and shall obtain the assistance of the LSBA Loss Prevention Counsel and the Louisiana State Bar Association's Practice Assistance Counsel in the creation of a proper law office management program.

C.  Respondent shall enroll in and attend one (1) full day of Ethics School administered by the Louisiana State Bar Association's Practice Assistance and Improvement Committee. 

D.  Respondent shall maintain current in the law during his period of probation by satisfying all annual MCLE requirements of the Louisiana State Bar Association in a timely fashion, pay all Louisiana State Bar Association bar dues, and pay all Louisiana Attorney Disciplinary Board disciplinary assessments imposed by the Louisiana Supreme Court. 

E. Respondent agrees that he will not violate the Rules of Professional Conduct while on probation.

F. Respondent will also respond to all reasonable requests of his probation monitor. 

APPLICATION OF FACTORS CONSIDERED IN IMPOSING SANCTIONS 

Respondent and ODC have agreed that Respondent has violated Rules 1.3, 1.7, and 3.2 of the Rules of Professional Conduct.  The Board must accept that Respondent has violated these rules.   In re Lieberman, 95-2628 (La. 6/7/96); 675 So. 2d 272. Given the violations of these rules, the Board is left to determine if the proposed sanction of public reprimand is appropriate. 

Louisiana Supreme Court Rule XIX, Section 10(C) states that in imposing a sanction after a finding of lawyer misconduct, the Court or Board shall consider the following factors: 

(1) whether the lawyer has violated a duty owed to a client, to the public, to the legal system, or to the profession; 

(2) whether the lawyer acted intentionally, knowingly, or negligently; 

(3) the amount of actual or potential injury caused by the lawyer's misconduct; and 

(4) the existence of any aggravating or mitigating factors. 

The record in this matter supports a finding that Respondent knowingly violated duties owed to his client. While the record fails to provide any solid evidence of the harm suffered by Respondent's client, the potential for injury always exists when an attorney fails to exercise due diligence or continues representation after a conflict arises.

The record supports a finding that the following mitigating factors are present in this matter: absence of a prior disciplinary record ,[v] remorse ,[vi] and cooperative attitude toward disciplinary proceedings .[vii]   Additionally, the record supports a finding that aggravating factors are not present in this matter. 

ANALYSIS 

I. ABA Standards  

The Louisiana Supreme Court relies on the ABA's Standards for Imposing Lawyer Sanction (herein "Standard") to determine the baseline sanction by determining "the type of duty violated, the lawyer's mental state and the extent of the injury caused; and then adjust[ing] the sanction in accordance with the aggravating and mitigating factors present."  In re Quaid, 94-1316 (La. 11/30/94); 646 So. 2d 343, 350.  

The proposed sanction is in line with the ABA guidelines.  Under A.B.A. Standard 4.43 , a "reprimand is generally appropriate when a lawyer is negligent and does not act with reasonable diligence in representing a client, and causes injury or potential injury to the client."  Under Standard 4.33 , a "reprimand is generally appropriate when a lawyer is negligent in determining whether the representation of a client may be materially affected by the lawyer's own interests, . . . and causes injury or potential injury to a client."  Standard 6.23 provides that a "reprimand is generally appropriate when a lawyer negligently fails to comply with a court order or rule, and causes injury or potential injury to a client . . . or causes interference with or potential interference with a legal proceeding."

II.   Relevant Case Law  

The Board also looks to relevant case law for assistance in determining an appropriate sanction for violations of the Rules of Professional Conduct. The case of In re Laiche, 98-DB-057, offers guidance here.  In Laiche, formal charges were filed against the respondent consisting of five counts that alleged violations of Rules of Professional Conduct 1.3, 1.4(a)(b), 1.16(d), 8.1(c) and 8.4(g).  After the respondent admitted to violating Rules 1.3, 1.4 and l.16(d), the parties later filed a joint motion for consent discipline proposing a public reprimand as the appropriate discipline.  Bd. Op. at 1. 

Count I of the formal charges brought against Laiche alleged that the respondent filed suit on behalf of his clients in a personal injury matter but did little more to move the case forward over the next eight years, in violation of Rule 1.3.  Count II of the charges alleged that Laiche failed to keep his clients informed as to the progress or lack of progress of the case, in violation of Rule 1.4(a).  The other pertinent counts alleged that the respondent failed to timely inform his clients of a settlement offer, which he rejected without consulting his clients, and failed to return a file and to timely sign a motion to substitute counsel to clients who had discharged him. Id.. at 1-2.  

The only aggravating factor was respondent's substantial experience in the practice of law.  Mitigating factors included the absence of a disciplinary record, no dishonest or selfish motive and a timely good faith effort to rectify the misconduct.  Id. at 2.  The Board accepted the joint petition, ordering that the respondent receive a public reprimand and that he be assessed with all costs and expenses of the proceedings.   In reaching its determination, the Board emphasized the fact that the clients did not suffer any actual harm.  Id. at 3. 

Another case that lends guidance is In re Brown, 00-DB-047.  In that case, the respondent was issued a public reprimand for violations of Rules of Professional Conduct 1.3 and 1.4(a).  There, the respondent received the file of a client from his former associate who had been handling the matter for several years.  Although the client was notified in writing of the transfer of the file, the respondent did nothing substantive to move the matter forward or to keep the client informed as to the status of the case for over a year.  The only aggravating factor present was the substantial experience in the practice of law.  The absence of a prior disciplinary record was cited as a mitigating factor.  Bd. Op. at 2-3. 

In In re Ruddick, 94-DB-060, formal charges were filed against the Respondent alleging violations of Rules of Professional Conduct 1.3, 1.4, 1.16 and 8.4(g).  There, the Respondent failed to provide his clients with a status report regarding a succession matter, neglected to complete the succession and failed to communicate with his clients.  He also failed to return documents to the clients and failed to remit an unearned fee.  The Respondent also failed to respond to inquiries from ODC concerning the clients' complaint.  Prior to the hearing scheduled in the matter, the Respondent filed a motion for consent discipline requesting that he receive a public reprimand for his misconduct.  Bd. Op. at pp. 1-2.  ODC concurred, and the Board approved the discipline.  Mitigating factors included no prior disciplinary record and personal problems.  Aggravating factors included multiple offenses and substantial experience in the practice of law.  Id. at 3-4.

 ODC cites the case of In re Dolbear, 98-DB-061 in support of the proposed sanction.  In that case, the respondent was retained in a matter involving four imprisoned Mexican nationals and accepted money to obtain their release.  He agreed to refund the money if he was unsuccessful.  When he was unsuccessful, he failed to return the fee.  In another matter, Dolbear agreed to review a file but failed to return it after the client decided not to retain him.  He also failed to cooperate with ODC in its investigation of the complaints.

The Board found that Dolbear negligently failed to act with reasonable diligence, negligently handled client property, and failed to cooperate with ODC. Numerous aggravating factors were cited by the Board, including prior misconduct, failure to cooperate, vulnerability of victim and substantial experience in the practice of law.  The mitigating factor of absence of selfish motive was also noted.  The respondent admitted that he violated Rules of Professional Conduct 1.3 , 1.4 , 1.5 , 1.16(d) , 8.1 and 8.4(a)(d)(g) .  The Board accepted the proposed consent discipline of a public reprimand.  Bd. Op. at pp. 1-2.   

Finally, another noteworthy case cited by ODC in support of the sanction is In re Dunn, 713 So.2d 461 (La. 1988).  The facts of Dunn, however, are more egregious than those seen in the matter at hand.  In Dunn, the respondent was retained to pursue a personal injury case arising out of vehicular accident.  Although he timely filed suit, he failed to actively pursue the suit for more than five years, resulting in dismissal of the suit for abandonment.  Dunn acknowledged his error to the client and offered to settle with the client.  He did not, however, advise his client to seek advice from independent counsel on the settlement.  The hearing committee found that Dunn was not trying to take advantage of his client.  Id. at 463.  The Court imposed a suspension of six months, deferred, along with one year of probation.  Id. at 464. 

III.  Conclusion  

This matter is akin to the above-cited cases in that the respondents have all engaged in conduct involving a lack of diligence or failure to expedite litigation. Given the fact that only one mitigating factor, lack of prior discipline, has been cited by the parties, and the fact that a violation of Rule 1.7 (conflict of interest) also has been agreed to by the parties, the imposition of a reprimand along with twelve months probation appears to be appropriate. 

RULING 

Based on the foregoing, it is ordered that the Amended Petition for Consent Discipline be accepted, and that a public reprimand be issued against the Respondent, Wayne Jollio James. Respondent is also ordered to serve a twelve-month period of probation subject to the following conditions:

A. Respondent shall establish and maintain an effective calendaring system and method to communicate with clients and shall obtain the assistance of the LSBA Loss Prevention Counsel and the Louisiana State Bar Association's Practice Assistance Counsel in the creation of a proper law office management program. 

B. Respondent shall enroll in and attend one (1) full day of Ethics School administered by the Louisiana State Bar Association's Practice Assistance and Improvement Committee. 

C. Respondent shall maintain current in the law during his period of probation by satisfying all annual MCLE requirements of the Louisiana State Bar Association in a timely fashion, pay all Louisiana State Bar Association bar dues, and pay all Louisiana Attorney Disciplinary Board disciplinary assessments imposed by the Louisiana Supreme Court. 

D. Respondent agrees that he will not violate the Rules of Professional Conduct while on probation. 

E.  Respondent will also respond to all reasonable requests of his probation monitor. 

It is further ordered that Respondent be assessed the costs and expenses of these proceedings, with legal interest to commence running thirty days from the date of finality of this Ruling until paid.

[i] Apparently, the conflict arose when the respondent decided not to oppose the dispositive motions in order to avoid the imposition of sanctions requested in the motion to compel.

[ii] Rule 1.3 is entitled "Diligence" and provides: "A lawyer shall act with reasonable diligence and promptness in representing a client."

[iii] Rule 1.7 is entitled "Conflict of Interest: General Rule" and provides:

"Loyalty is an essential element in the lawyer's relationship to a client.  Therefore:

"(a) A lawyer shall not represent a client if the representation of that client will be directly adverse to another client, unless:

"(1) The lawyer reasonably believes the representation will not adversely affect the relationship with the other client; and

"(2) Each client consents after consultation.

"(b) A lawyer shall not represent a client if the representation of that client may be materially limited by the lawyer's responsibilities  to another client or to a third person, or by the lawyer's own interests, unless;

"(1) The lawyer reasonably believes the representation will not be adversely affected; and

"(2) The client consents after consultation.  When representation of multiple clients in a single matter is undertaken, the consultation shall include explanation of the implications of the common representation and the advantages and risks involved."

[iv] Rule 3.2 is entitled "Expediting Litigation" and provides: "A lawyer shall make reasonable efforts to expedite litigation consistent with the interests of the client."
